# Natural Fabrics

Natural fabrics come from plant or animal sources. In these notes, the key natural fibres are cotton, wool and silk.

<div class="fibre-family natural" markdown>
  <section>
    <h2>Cotton</h2>
    <p>Cotton is a natural cellulosic fibre containing about 80-90 percent cellulose. It is popular because it is comfortable, absorbent, versatile and suitable for warm weather.</p>
  </section>
  <section>
    <h2>Wool</h2>
    <p>Wool is a natural protein fibre obtained from the fleece of sheep and other animals such as goats, llamas, alpacas and vicunas. It is valued for warmth, resilience and insulation.</p>
  </section>
  <section>
    <h2>Silk</h2>
    <p>Silk is a natural protein filament fibre made by silkworms. It is known as the queen of fibres because of its lustre, softness, strength and drape.</p>
  </section>
</div>

## Cotton

### Manufacturing process

1. Cultivation: cotton plants blossom, form cotton balls, and mature until the balls open to reveal white fibre.
2. Ginning: seeds are removed from harvested cotton.
3. Spinning: cotton fibre is converted into yarn.
4. Weaving: two yarn sets are interlaced to produce fabric.

### Properties and uses

| Property | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Strength | Cotton is fairly strong and becomes about 20 percent stronger when wet. |
| Limited elasticity | It does not stretch much and has low recovery from deformation. |
| Moisture absorbency | Absorbs water and sweat, making it comfortable in summer. |
| Thermal conductivity | Allows heat to dissipate, helping the wearer stay cool. |
| Chemical resistance | Resistant to alkalis but damaged by acids. |
| Biodegradability | Breaks down naturally as a plant-based fibre. |

## Wool

### Manufacturing process

1. Shearing collects fleece from sheep.
2. Skirting and sorting remove undesirable parts and group fibre by length, diameter and colour.
3. Scouring washes away dirt, grease and impurities.
4. Carbonising removes vegetable impurities using acid treatment.
5. Carding aligns fibres into a web.
6. Combing may remove shorter fibres for higher quality yarn.
7. Spinning twists fibres into yarn.
8. Weaving or knitting turns yarn into fabric.
9. Finishing adds washing, dyeing and desired treatments.

### Properties and uses

| Property | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Crimp | Wave-like structure gives elasticity and insulation. |
| Softness | Comfortable next to skin, depending on animal breed and fibre quality. |
| Moisture absorption | Helps keep skin dry and comfortable. |
| Insulation | Warm in cold weather and comfortable across temperatures. |
| Flame resistance | Naturally safer than many fibres around flame. |
| Durability | Strong enough for garments and textiles subject to wear. |
| Biodegradability | Breaks down naturally in the environment. |

Wool can be made into woollens from shorter fibres and worsteds from longer fibres. Worsteds are finer and often used for expensive suiting.

## Silk

### Manufacturing process

1. Sericulture: care and cultivation of silkworms.
2. Reeling: filament is taken from cocoons.
3. Throwing: reeled silk strands are twisted or doubled into yarn.
4. Degumming: sericin gum is removed to improve softness and lustre.

### Properties and uses

| Property | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Softness | Smooth handle suited to garments and bedding. |
| Strength | Strong despite its fine and soft feel. |
| Shine | Natural lustre gives a luxurious appearance. |
| Thermal regulation | Cool in warm weather and warm in cool weather. |
| Hypoallergenic character | Useful for people with sensitive skin. |
| Dyeability | Accepts rich, vibrant colours well. |
| Drapability | Falls beautifully while retaining shape. |
| Moisture wicking | Breathable and comfortable in warm weather. |
