# Synthetic Fabrics

This section compares regenerated and synthetic fibres from the notes: rayon, nylon, polyester and acrylic. Rayon is regenerated from cellulose, while nylon, polyester and acrylic are synthetic polymer fibres.

## Fibre comparison

| Fibre | Fibre family | Key identity | Strong design cue |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rayon | Regenerated cellulosic | Cellulose is chemically reformed into fibre. | Soft, absorbent, lustrous and drapes well, but weak when wet. |
| Nylon | Synthetic polyamide | First commercially successful synthetic fibre, developed in 1935. | Strong, elastic, abrasion resistant and quick drying. |
| Polyester | Synthetic PET | Made from polyethylene terephthalate. | Durable, wrinkle resistant, quick drying and cost effective. |
| Acrylic | Synthetic polyacrylonitrile | Wool-like fibre with warmth and low weight. | Soft, warm, resilient and sunlight resistant. |

## Rayon

Rayon is made from regenerated cellulose from sources such as wood pulp. It was the first regenerated fibre and has been sold as artificial silk. Viscose rayon is the most common type; other types include high wet modulus rayon, lyocell and cuprammonium rayon.

### Viscose process

1. Wood pulp is prepared and purified into cellulose sheets.
2. Cellulose is steeped in sodium hydroxide.
3. The sheets are shredded into crumbs.
4. The crumbs are aged under controlled conditions.
5. Carbon disulfide converts cellulose into cellulose xanthate.
6. The compound dissolves into viscose solution.
7. Wet spinning through spinnerets regenerates cellulose into solid fibre.
8. Fibres are washed, bleached, dried and cut or kept as filaments.

### Properties and uses

Rayon is soft, absorbent, breathable, lustrous, dyeable and has good drape. It also wrinkles easily, burns readily, can be damaged by strong chemicals and may lose 30-50 percent of strength when wet.

## Nylon

Nylon is a synthetic polyamide fibre developed by Wallace Carothers and his team at DuPont in 1935 and introduced commercially in 1938. Common types include Nylon 6 and Nylon 6,6.

### Manufacturing process

1. Monomers are prepared: Nylon 6,6 uses hexamethylenediamine and adipic acid; Nylon 6 uses caprolactam.
2. Polymerisation forms long-chain polyamide molecules.
3. Melt spinning extrudes molten polymer through spinnerets.
4. Drawing stretches filaments and aligns polymer chains.
5. Texturing, dyeing and finishing create the required performance and appearance.

### Properties and uses

Nylon has high tensile strength, excellent elasticity, abrasion resistance, chemical resistance, low moisture absorption and a lustrous or matte finish. It is used in hosiery, swimwear, activewear, outerwear, airbags, seatbelts, upholstery, ropes, conveyor belts, fishing nets, toothbrush bristles, guitar strings and sporting equipment.

## Polyester

Polyester is a synthetic fibre derived from PET, produced by polymerising ethylene glycol and terephthalic acid. It became widely used because it is strong, lightweight, durable, wrinkle resistant and cost effective.

### Manufacturing process

1. Raw materials are prepared.
2. Polymerisation forms long-chain polymers.
3. Melt spinning extrudes filaments through spinnerets.
4. Drawing and stretching improve strength and elasticity.
5. Texturing adds bulk or insulation.
6. Finishing may include heat setting, dyeing, water repellency or flame resistance treatments.

### Properties and uses

Polyester is strong, resilient, hydrophobic, quick drying, thermally stable, chemically resistant, abrasion resistant, UV resistant, mildew resistant and has strong dye retention. It is also non-biodegradable, so recycling through rPET is important.

## Acrylic

Acrylic fibre is made mainly from polyacrylonitrile. It became popular as a wool substitute because it is soft, warm, light in weight and wool-like in appearance.

### Manufacturing process

1. Acrylonitrile is polymerised with selected co-monomers.
2. The polymer dissolves into spinning dope.
3. Wet or dry spinning forms filaments through spinnerets.
4. Drawing and washing improve strength and remove solvent.
5. Crimping and heat setting add bulk, resilience and dimensional stability.
6. Continuous filaments may be cut into staple lengths.

### Properties and uses

Acrylic has moderate strength, excellent resilience, low moisture absorbency, quick drying behaviour, good acid and dry-cleaning solvent resistance, low weight, warmth, biological resistance and excellent sunlight resistance. It can be highly inflammable, so care and end use matter.
